Consciousness

The definition

The word consciousness derives from the Latin conscio, where the roots cum and scio respectively mean ‘with or together’ and ‘know’. Originally, its meaning had three different nuances:

1.the concept of knowledge

2.the act of being conscious of something with someone else

3. and self-consciousness, where someone is conscious with oneself, i.e. the mind.

Nowadays, the terms ‘consciousness‘ and ‘conscious’ have respectively ten and fifteen denotations, all falling in three broad categories or usages.

According to the first usage, being conscious is equivalent to being awake, alert and active, as opposed to sleepy on in coma. In this case, consciousness can be extended to all living organisms showing wakefulness, which can be temporary lost during sleep or permanently in death. It can be argued that we cannot detect such states in organisms lacking a nervous system such as unicellular and multicellular placozoans and jellyfish (although these have a diffuse nerve net). Here, we can already see that being awake and alive are very different concepts, stemming from as much different biological entities respectively being the nervous system and the cell.

The second category views consciousness as the affective and idiosyncratic feature of experience. It is the emotional and subjective state that accompanies the reminiscence of a specific memory or dictates our daily non-verbal communication. As opposed to the first usage, the second does not encompass all brain-possessing animals. In fact, all mammals, birds, and many other creatures, including octopuses have the neurological substrates underlying affective consciousness.

Last, the third class of interpretations leads to the denotation of the mind, where thoughts, beliefs, habits, and memories converge. This last category is primarily distinctive of human beings, as its neurobiological underpinnings lie mostly within the neocortex, which constitutes 80 % of the human brain.
